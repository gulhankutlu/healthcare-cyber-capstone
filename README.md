# Healthcare Cybersecurity Governance Capstone

## Cybersecurity Governance and Security Policy Framework for a Fictional Healthcare Organization

This repository presents my Master’s-level cybersecurity capstone project, developed as part of my graduate studies at Strayer University.

The project was completed throughout the academic term through a series of integrated cybersecurity assessments, security architecture designs, incident response plans, governance policies, and compliance recommendations.

It focuses on designing a comprehensive cybersecurity governance, risk management, and security policy framework for a fictional nonprofit healthcare organization operating in a hybrid technology environment.

> **Academic and No-Affiliation Disclaimer:** The healthcare organization, systems, incidents, vulnerabilities, and policies described in this project are entirely fictional and were developed exclusively for academic purposes. This project is not affiliated with, endorsed by, sponsored by, or based on any actual healthcare organization. It does not contain real patient information, protected health information, confidential business data, production credentials, or information belonging to an actual organization.

## Project Overview

Healthcare organizations manage highly sensitive information while relying on interconnected clinical systems, cloud services, telemedicine platforms, remote access technologies, connected medical devices, and third-party vendors.

This project evaluates cybersecurity risks affecting a fictional healthcare organization and proposes a structured governance and security policy framework aligned with recognized cybersecurity standards, regulatory requirements, and industry guidance.

The project integrates risk assessment, technical security controls, incident response, business continuity, artificial intelligence governance, regulatory compliance, and organizational accountability within a unified cybersecurity program.

## Project Objectives

- Establish cybersecurity governance, accountability, and clearly defined security responsibilities.
- Identify organizational risks, vulnerabilities, threats, and security control gaps.
- Strengthen identity and access management.
- Improve network segmentation, endpoint security, and remote access protection.
- Protect sensitive healthcare and organizational information.
- Develop incident response and ransomware preparedness procedures.
- Support business continuity and disaster recovery planning.
- Address third-party, vendor, cloud, and supply-chain risks.
- Establish artificial intelligence governance policies.
- Support regulatory compliance, security auditing, and continuous improvement.

## Fictional Organization Scenario

The project is based on a fictional nonprofit healthcare organization providing in-person clinical and telemedicine services.

Its technology environment includes:

- Electronic health record and electronic medical record systems
- Hybrid cloud infrastructure
- Remote workforce access
- Virtual private network connectivity
- Third-party telemedicine platforms
- Clinical and administrative workstations
- Business-critical healthcare applications
- Connected medical and Internet of Medical Things devices
- Vendors with access to organizational systems and information
- Artificial intelligence technologies supporting clinical and administrative activities

The organization’s principal cybersecurity concerns include inconsistent access controls, insufficient network segmentation, third-party exposure, encryption gaps, ransomware risk, remote access vulnerabilities, legacy technology, and the absence of formal artificial intelligence governance.

## Layered Security Architecture

The proposed security architecture follows a defense-in-depth approach and includes:

- Firewall and intrusion detection and prevention protections
- Network segmentation for clinical, administrative, guest, vendor, and device environments
- Virtual private network access with multi-factor authentication
- Role-based access control
- Least-privilege permissions
- Account lockout and authentication protections
- Endpoint detection and response
- Device compliance monitoring
- Centralized security event logging
- Threat investigation and incident escalation
- Quarantine procedures for compromised devices
- Indicator-of-compromise analysis
- Encryption for sensitive data in transit and at rest
- Secure backup and recovery controls

This layered approach reduces dependence on any single security control and limits the potential impact of compromised users, endpoints, applications, or third-party services.

## Identity and Access Management

The identity and access management strategy includes:

- Multi-factor authentication
- Role-based access control
- Least-privilege access
- Centralized identity management
- Secure remote access
- Privileged account separation
- Periodic access reviews
- Account provisioning and deprovisioning
- Session and credential revocation
- Authentication monitoring
- Device trust and compliance validation

These controls reduce the risk of unauthorized access resulting from phishing, stolen credentials, excessive privileges, inactive accounts, or compromised devices.

## Artificial Intelligence Governance

The project includes three primary artificial intelligence governance areas.

### Clinical Oversight and Accountability

AI-supported recommendations must remain subject to appropriate human review. Clinical and organizational leaders retain responsibility for decisions affecting patients, employees, and business operations.

AI systems must not replace licensed clinical judgment or make high-impact decisions without appropriate oversight.

### Data Governance and Privacy

Data used by AI systems must be:

- Authorized for the intended purpose
- Appropriately classified
- Accurately maintained
- Limited according to minimum-necessary principles
- Protected through access controls and encryption
- De-identified when appropriate
- Retained only for approved periods
- Securely disposed of when no longer required
- Monitored through audit trails

Protected health information must not be used for AI development, training, or testing without appropriate authorization and safeguards.

### AI Model Lifecycle Management

AI systems must be evaluated throughout their lifecycle, including:

- Planning and approval
- Data selection
- Development or acquisition
- Clinical and technical validation
- Bias and fairness assessment
- Security testing
- Deployment
- Performance monitoring
- Data and model drift detection
- Change management
- Incident investigation
- Retraining and revalidation
- Retirement and secure decommissioning

These policies address human oversight, privacy, model monitoring, security testing, bias risk, data authorization, model manipulation, accountability, and responsible AI use.

## Implementation Roadmap

The proposed implementation roadmap is divided into three phases.

### Phase 1: Stabilize Critical Risk

- Require multi-factor authentication for sensitive and privileged access.
- Identify critical assets, data, accounts, integrations, and dependencies.
- Validate offline or immutable backups.
- Review remote access and firewall exposure.
- Establish ransomware escalation procedures.
- Disable stale accounts and separate privileged access.
- Assign executive sponsors and policy owners.

### Phase 2: Build Consistent Control Coverage

- Implement segmented trust zones.
- Expand endpoint detection and centralized logging.
- Formalize access reviews and account lifecycle processes.
- Standardize encryption and data classification.
- Complete high-risk vendor assessments.
- Establish formal artificial intelligence oversight.

### Phase 3: Mature, Measure, and Optimize

- Measure control effectiveness.
- Expand Zero Trust capabilities.
- Integrate vendor, cloud, device, and AI environments into security monitoring.
- Conduct cross-functional incident and recovery exercises.
- Monitor AI systems for drift, bias, security issues, and performance degradation.
- Maintain a documented cybersecurity improvement backlog.
